Passive Components

Energy-Storing and Dissipating Devices

Passive components cannot generate power or amplify electrical signals. Instead, they store, release, or dissipate the electrical energy already moving through a circuit. This is primarily done by manipulating geometric structures and material choices to alter electrostatic fields, electric and magnetic flux, or thermal properties. Essentially, we pass current through conductive loops or resistive paths, which opposes flow, storing energy within localized magnetic fields, or separates net charges across dielectric barriers. This physics allows passive elements to safely store energy in the form of electric / magnetic fields, aswell as balance voltages and currents without needing an auxiliary power supply.

The Main Passive Components:

Capacitors
Two-terminal passive devices consisting of conductive plates that store electrical energy within an electrostatic field. To study these electric fields further, you can read more about basic electrical capacitance mechanics on our deep-dive module page.
Inductors
Coiled passive components that resist changes in current by temporarily storing energy inside an induced magnetic field.
Resistors
Two-terminal components engineered purely to reduce electrical current flow and drop voltage by converting energy into thermal dissipation.
